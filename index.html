By the Fracker Hackers
"The first day of classes is just over a week away, but the beginning of the semester can be seen across campus and with it the steps we are taking together to keep one another safe."

These were the confident words that Bob Blouin, Executive Vice Chancellor and Provost of the University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill safely declared before the beginning of the public university's fall 2020 semester. The provost held high hopes for the safety of students in the upcoming school year.

"[The university has decided] to shift our undergraduate instruction entirely remote."

After nearly a week of classes, UNC confirmed 88 cases of students testing positive for COVID-19 on the same day that North Carolina reached its highest peak of 2,007 cases since late July.

More than Just UNC

Other universities faced similar closures. After two weeks of classes, Notre Dame shifted its in-person classes to remote learning after amassing 147 total positive cases. While other universities such as Stanford University, Temple University, and North Carolina State University quickly committed to remote learning before the start of their academic semesters, many US universities - despite obvious danger - pushed to reopen their schools and welcomed back their students.

The consequences of these decisions are evident in publicly available testing data. But below are some highlights.

Why the concern over universities?

Not only do rising cases risk the lives of students, but tight-packed universities incubate COVID-19 in a way that affects state cases of COVID-19 at large.

Positive cases fluctuations at several major universities mirror upticks in cases in their respective states.

  • On September 16, Arizona approached its highest positive case count since July at 698 cases.
  • On August 20, North Carolina experienced a surge of cases at 2007 cases.
  • On August 23, North Dakota experienced its greatest spike of the summer at 526 cases.
  • On September 4, Illinois cases surged to 5287 positive cases.
  • On September 11, California cases surged to 4107 positive cases.
  • Arizona State University experienced its highest number of cases since the beginning of the school eyar at 245 cases./li>
  • NC Chapel Hill began experiencing its highest number of cases (resulting in its closure) at 88 cases.
  • A day later, North Dakota University experienced its greatest number of positive cases at 69 cases.
  • The University of Illinois Urbana Champaign reported a high of 104 cases the same day.
  • The University of California, Berkeley reported its high of 12 cases the same day.

Is this actually alarming? Maybe UNC and Notre Dame were isolated cases.

Many in-person and hybrid universities are grappling similar problems currently and are developing conditions where per capita cases far exceed per capita cases in countless countries worldwide.

What role do states play?

Aside from COVID-19 reactions, early and strict government policies demonstrate fewer cases over time. If state governments take action to properly regulate public universities by encouraging safe practices, universities will be better equipped to provide safe (alternatives to) education for students.


States and colleges' cases are interrelated, and states should take a closer look at taking care of students and their universities' policies.

Why were we interested?

We're students interested in understanding data that makes an impact. Temporal, continuous data for an aggregate of US colleges and universities do not exist. We scraped and hand-picked data from university websites to compile these original datasets and better understand trends. These trends can help us understand the connection between states cases and college cases and inform decisions regarding student safety.
